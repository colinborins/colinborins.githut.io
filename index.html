The Challange

Understanding empathetic responses can be challenging, especially when discussing empathy. The “Appraisal Theory of Empathy and Other Vicarious Emotional Experiences” explores the meaning of empathy and empathic emotions. As explained in the article, empathy is defined as feeling what someone else is feeling. Empathy differs from emotional theory - it does not have the same characteristics. Emotional theory, stated by Wondra and Ellsworth, is characterized by the presence of compassion. This is explained in the passage, “[t]he essence of empathy, agreed upon by most empathy researchers, is feeling what another person feels because something happens to them, and that is what we mean by empathy in our discussions. This conceptualization of empathy is equivalent to affective resonance or experience sharing in multicomponent models of empathy, but it does not include other processes that some empathy theorists believe contribute to emotion sharing, such as perspective taking, self-regulation, and mind perception” (Wondra and Ellsworth 411). The journal, Empathy and Other Vicarious Emotional Experiences empathy, discusses the difference between compassion and empathy; “you can feel sad with someone else who is sad (empathy) and also feel concern (compassion), you can also feel happy with someone who is happy (empathy) and feel no concern because nothing bad has happened. Our use of the term empathy does not require understanding another’s internal states” (Wondra and Ellsworth, 412).Empathy compared to emotion theory and empathetic responses are shown to have different characteristics. Unlike Empathy that doesn't directly address emotions, empathic responses are a combination of empathic and emotional theory. Joshua D. Wondra and Phoebe C. Ellsworth passage explains this, “[t]hese theories address how our own emotions are affected by the emotions of others. They do not address our capacity to feel emotions for others when we are not in the same situation or when we are not members of the same group. Empathy theories do discuss our capacity to feel emotions for others, but they treat empathy as something special and they do not relate empathy to the processes that drive firsthand emotional experiences” (Wondra and Ellsworth 412).
